The 400-year-old secret

You weren’t the first to suspect there was a trick to it.

For centuries, the uncanny accuracy of certain masterworks raised a quiet question: were the great realists working from the eye alone — or with help from optics? The historical record points to a family of tools that bend and reflect light so an artist can trace exactly what’s in front of them.

~1000 - present

The camera obscura

A darkened room or box projects the scene outside through a small aperture — the oldest optical drawing aid. Studied by Alhazen, da Vinci, and countless artists since.

1807, patented by W.H. Wollaston

The camera lucida

A prism (and later, a mirror) lays a ghost image of your subject right onto the paper. You see the scene and your pencil at once — and trace. The LUCY is a modern take on this exact instrument.

The artist David Hockney’s Secret Knowledge argued that optical aids were in use far earlier and more widely than art history had admitted. The documentary Tim’s Vermeer followed one inventor as he reconstructed how a mirror-based setup could produce a Vermeer-like image.

Hockney, “Secret Knowledge” (2001) · “Tim’s Vermeer” (2013) — cited as cultural touchstones for the debate.
An honest note on the history

This is a genuinely debated subject, so we put it straight: optical aids have been associated with the Old Masters — Vermeer, Rembrandt, Ingres, and others — and the theory suggests their precision may have leaned on them. Scholars still disagree on who used what. What isn’t in doubt is that the camera lucida is a real, documented instrument with a 200-plus-year pedigree — and the LUCY is its modern descendant.

The question every thoughtful artist asks

“Isn’t that cheating?”

It’s the fair, honest hesitation — and the answer is worth thinking through, because it changes how you’ll feel every time you sit down with it.

The worry

“A tool means it doesn’t really count.”

The fear is that the device does the art for you — that you haven’t earned the result, and you won’t actually learn anything.

The reality

It’s a learning aid — you still draw every stroke.

It helps you see. You still draw every single stroke yourself — every line, every shadow, every choice. It trains the one skill self-taught artists fight hardest: seeing proportion accurately.

The masters didn’t apologize for their tools — neither should you.

Brushes, grids, lenses, mirrors, the rules of perspective — every era’s artists used the best instruments available to get the truth of a subject onto a surface. The camera lucida was one of them. Using a recognized optical aid doesn’t place you outside that tradition; it places you squarely inside it.

  • You compose, you draw, you shade — the artistry is entirely yours.
  • Many artists use it as a feedback loop — draw, compare, learn — and lean on it less as their eye sharpens.
  • It trains your eye for proportion and perspective — results vary with practice.

Quick answers

The questions a careful buyer asks

Is it really the same tool the Old Masters used?
It’s the same family of optical instrument — the camera lucida, a documented tool patented in 1807 and part of a lineage of optical aids that have been associated with master artists. Historians still debate exactly who used what. The LUCY is a modern, mirror-based take on that instrument; we don’t claim a specific master used this specific device.
Is using it cheating?
No — it’s a recognized learning aid. The LUCY does the seeing; you draw every stroke yourself, and over time it trains your eye for proportion. Using a recognized optical tool puts you inside the tradition of artists who used the best instruments of their day, not outside it.
Will it work in normal room light?
Yes, at an ordinary well-lit desk — but because it shows a reflection (not a projected beam), lighting matters. Light your subject well and keep your paper a touch dimmer. The flex and pro include multiple brightness levels to dial it in. You don’t need a dark room.
I wear glasses — is that a problem?
Single-vision glasses are fine. Progressives or bifocals can sometimes make the image appear doubled, because you’re looking at two focal distances at once. The easy fix: wear inexpensive single-vision readers while you draw, or remove your glasses if you can focus at drawing distance.
