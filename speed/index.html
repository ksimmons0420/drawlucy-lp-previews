Two quick honest answers

The two things experienced painters ask

“Doesn’t this feel like cheating?”

It’s the same idea the Old Masters leaned on. Optical drawing aids — the camera lucida and camera obscura — have been associated with master artists like Vermeer and Rembrandt for centuries. The LUCY is a modern take on that technique.

It does the seeing. You still paint every single stroke yourself — the colour, the values, the brushwork, the life. It just hands you an accurate layin so your skill goes into the painting, not the plotting.

A learning aid, not a crutch

“Is it fiddly to set up?”

Setup is about 30 seconds: clamp it to your table or easel, aim it, and dial the brightness. From there it stays put for the whole session.

Honest note: there’s a short learning curve the first time — budget one short practice session to get your eye in the right spot. After that, most artists call it effortless. (It’s a reflection, not a projector, so a well-lit desk helps; the flex and pro have multiple brightness levels.)

Quick answers

For the working painter

How fast can I really get a layin down?
Once it’s set up — about 30 seconds — the accurate layin takes minutes instead of the hour that measuring and gridding can eat. There’s a short learning curve the first time you use it; after that most artists describe it as effortless. Results vary with practice.
Can I paint over the layin, or just draw?
You can use any medium on virtually any surface — pencil, charcoal, pen, marker, and paint on paper or canvas. Lay the composition in accurately, lift the LUCY, and paint your way. Most LUCY buyers are painters and drawers, not beginners.
Does it work from my own photos?
Yes. The LUCY draws from live 3D subjects natively, and works from photos too. To enlarge a photo onto a bigger canvas, most artists pair it with the $22 Photo Enlarger accessory (sizes a print up 2.5–4×).
I wear glasses — will it work?
Single-vision glasses are fine. Progressives or bifocals can sometimes make the reflected image appear doubled — the easy fix is to wear inexpensive single-vision readers while you draw, or remove your glasses if you can focus at drawing distance.
